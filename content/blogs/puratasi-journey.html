The Village Custom

The Tamil month of Purattasi (September - October) traditionally means avoiding non-vegetarian food and intensely worshipping Lord Vishnu, often by visiting his temples. This was a direct lesson taught by my village environment; in fact, the Govindharaja Perumal Temple was literally behind my house. Because it was a strong family tradition, I followed this custom for years without questioning it. However, we are fully having the believe on Lord Muruga and worshiping as well as Lord Vishnu.

The Unexpected Spark: The Kalki Story

My journey from simple to deep interest was started unexpectedly, not in a temple, it was in the entrance of the cinema hall. Me and my friends went to watch the part one of the Kalki movie. I was the only person in my group who knew nothing about the Mahabharata story - the very foundation of this movie, which is about the next Yuga. Exactly in front of the screen, one of my friends gave me a brief overview about the Mahabharata story. At the end of the movie, my friendтАЩs overview had sparked a fire! It inspired me to watch the entire Mahabharata series made in India.

A New Personal Quest

This year Puratasi month was born with my interest, which was sparked by my friendтАЩs story. But this time I have different queries within my mind. I need to understand one more level about Lord Vishnu and his duty, purely for myself. Basically, this is not about debating for believers vs non-believers. Also, this is not specifically why this particular month is special. This is literally for my own interest about devotion.

I know that this series might be slightly deviated from the actual Mahabharata which is composed by Veda Viyasa. But I watched it all, achieving a deep and thorough understanding. I realized a fundamental truth that highlighted in Mahabharata: whenever Adharmam (unrighteousness) reaches its peak, Lord Vishnu takes an Avataram (incarnation) to destroy that darkness and re-establish Dharmam (righteousness). This divine pattern has repeated across every Yuga (era).

Daily Routine in Chennai

With that newfound conviction about Lord Vishnu and a growing love for His grace, my Puratasi month journey officially began. Naturally, the routine had to change a bit since I am currently living in Chennai, away from my village. The basic framework of the fast might be the same, but the daily practice adapted to my city life. My days started with waking up early. Instead of traditional temple routines, I created a personal space for devotion. This involved listening to the Bhagavad Gita Slokas, any version I could find, and dedicating time to my favourite Carnatic singer, Sooryagayathri's songs related to Lord Vishnu.

My spiritual highlight, however, was visiting the Kothanda Ramar Temple in West Mambalam, especially on Saturdays. It was always an amazing and peaceful experience! Imagine this: sitting under the Arasa Maram (Peepal tree), feeling the cool air, and listening to the beautiful blend of 'Bhaja Govindam' sung by my favourite, Sooryagayathri, and the legendary M.S. Subbulakshmi! That serene combination - the sacred space, the gentle wind, and those divine voices - felt truly unparalleled. That was my routine, my personal way of embracing the month here in the city.

The Two Core Lessons from the Gita

As I immersed myself in the recordings and lessons of the Bhagavad Gita, two slokas immediately provided the philosophical foundation for my commitment. These weren't just verses; they were explanations for my life and my faith:

  • Yada Yada Hi Dharmasya (The Protector's Promise): This verse cemented my core belief. Simply put, it means that whenever goodness declines and evil rises, Lord Vishnu promises to take a form and appear to set things right. This realization gave me immense faith that there is always an ultimate restorer of order.

  • Karmani Vadhikaraste (The Lesson of Action): This second verse was my lesson in discipline and peace. It teaches us to focus entirely on the doing of the work, and not worry about what we will gain from it. I learned to find satisfaction in the process of devotion itself, rather than obsessing over the results.

Harmony in Devotion

My devotion this month was truly fueled by music. My playlist, filled with Carnatic songs by Sooryagayathri, became my daily temple. I constantly listened to favourites like 'Bhaja Govindam' and 'Rangapura Vihara', which are direct praises of Lord Vishnu.

But there was one song that held a special place: 'Chandrachooda'. It may seem surprising, as 'Chandrachooda' primarily praises Lord Shiva. However, this beautiful song is actually a testament to the harmony between the gods! It reveals that Lord Shiva is himself a great devotee of Lord Vishnu. This realization was very inspiring: even the fierce Lord Shiva bows down to Vishnu, constantly chanting his name. It showed me that true devotion transcends any specific deity and is about love and respect for the divine order. This realization added another layer of peace to my Puratasi journey.

This month was no longer a duty, but a joyful, self-driven quest for Dharma.
